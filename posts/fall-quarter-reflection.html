Fall quarter 2025 reflection

entry #2 • 2025-12-16 • 4 min read
reflection college growth
Notebook / reflections • Entry #2
2025-12-16 4 min read

This quarter was busy — but the kind of busy that teaches you things.

Going into fall quarter, I expected intensity. Seven classes, leadership roles across multiple student organizations, and uncertainty around my professional path meant I knew I would be stretched. What I didn’t expect was how much I would grow to enjoy that pace.

Somewhere between deadlines, late nights, and new conversations, the chaos started to feel grounding. Submitting the bulk of my transfer applications lifted a weight I had been carrying quietly for months. For the first time in a while, I could focus less on what was looming ahead and more on what was directly in front of me.

Expectations vs. reality

At the start of the quarter, I wasn’t sure what my professional situation would look like. Academically, I expected to be overloaded. Personally, I was excited to reconnect with friends and meet new people through clubs.

Reality exceeded those expectations. I joined KCC Capital Partners as a Pharma/Biotech Investment Banking Analyst, managed my coursework more effectively than I anticipated, and met a lot of new people who quickly became friends. The busyness didn’t feel draining — it felt purposeful.

Learning how I think

Chemistry was the hardest class for me. I’ve never been good at it, and that didn’t change. But other classes challenged me in more constructive ways.

As I went deeper into quantitative finance and research, I began internalizing a more systematic, probabilistic way of thinking — optimizing for expected value rather than certainty. That framework has quietly reshaped how I approach decisions far beyond markets or math.

In African American Studies, I was struck by how differently people could write about the same ideas. Navigating subjectivity — especially when there isn’t a single correct answer — forced me to engage with perspectives outside my usual way of thinking.

Responsibility at scale

Outside the classroom, the quarter was defined by responsibility. Alongside my role at KCC Capital Partners, I served on the executive board of De Anza Phi Beta Lambda, worked with TEDx De Anza, helped grow Polaris Research Group, and contributed to De Anza Hacks.

Phi Beta Lambda demanded the most. We ran a full recruiting cycle, took 60 members to San Diego for FBLA States, hosted our own Innovation Summit, and balanced consulting projects at the same time.

During midterms, everything converged — work deadlines, Polaris recruiting, and exams. I didn’t find a clever workaround. I just powered through and became more intentional about where my effort actually mattered.

Tradeoffs and clarity

This quarter clarified my direction. Another finance role, strong performance in math, and an invitation to the SIG Virtual Discovery Program reinforced that I’m moving in the right direction.

The tradeoff was sleep. Most nights ended after 1 a.m. I also stopped actively initiating hangouts and learned to say no more often. Opportunities would come — I didn’t need to chase all of them.

Wins, misses, and honesty

Hosting the De Anza Innovation Summit, organizing TEDx De Anza’s CC to Success panel, running DA Hacks 4.0, and leading FBLA States showed me something important: I can manage large teams and execute under pressure.

At the same time, this quarter surfaced a pattern I can’t ignore. I overcommit and sometimes sacrifice my own well-being. That’s something I need to change.

Looking ahead

Next quarter, I’m narrowing my focus. I’m not adding anything new. With a lighter academic load, I want to reclaim time for myself — sleeping properly, working out, and building habits that are sustainable.

You don’t need to say yes to everything. Put yourself first and build something you can sustain.
