work

a brief account of where i've been and what i was trying to do.

esplanade capital 2025
quantitative researcher · mbs convexity hedging

esplanade was where i first did quantitative research that genuinely mattered to an investment decision. the focus was mbs convexity hedging, a corner of fixed income that is technically dense and operationally unforgiving. prepayment models interact with rate dynamics in ways that are hard to disentangle, and the hedging problem is complicated precisely because the convexity profile of a mortgage pool shifts as rates move.

what i took away from that experience was less about the specific models and more about what it means to build something that a portfolio manager will actually rely on. the tolerance for elegant-but-wrong is close to zero. that pressure was useful.

aletheia laboratories 2024
head of applied science · aletheialaboratories.com

aletheia was built on a specific neuroscientific claim: that cognitive errors under pressure have detectable eeg correlates, and that those correlates could be read in time to be actionable. not a wellness product or a biofeedback toy. a system designed to surface what the brain knows before the trader does.

as head of applied science, i directed a small neuroscience research team working across the core technical problem. that meant owning the research agenda: what neural signatures we were targeting, how we were extracting and classifying them from noisy eeg data, and what the latency budget actually had to look like for any of it to matter in a live trading environment. the work sat at the intersection of cognitive neuroscience, signal processing, and applied machine learning, and the team had to be fluent in all three at once.

we shut down after about eight months when funding dried up before we could reach a proof of concept that would have changed the conversation. i think about aletheia as the place where i learned that the most interesting technical problems are rarely the ones that kill a company.

beus center for cosmic foundations 2023
computational astrophysics researcher · arizona state university

before finance, there was astrophysics. i spent time at asu's beus center training predictive models for solar flare forecasting, working on a problem that is genuinely hard: solar flares are rare, high-consequence events with complex magnetohydrodynamic precursors, and the data is noisy, imbalanced, and unforgiving to overfit on. building something that generalizes rather than memorizes required being careful in ways that most applied ml work does not demand.

i think about that experience now as the place where i learned to care about the difference between a model that produces the right output and a model that produces the right output for the right reasons. that distinction travels well.
